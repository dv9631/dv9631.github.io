      5.3 Failure mode, effects, and criticality analysis (FMECA)

      5.3.1 purpose of analysis

      1. qualitative measures of magnitude of failure mode effect
      2. impact or importance of a failure mode
      3. quantify the relative magnitude of each failure rate
      4. aid to decision making
      5. combination of criticality and severity,priority for action to mitigate or minimize effect of certain failures

      5.3.2 risk, R and risk priority number

      1. subjective measure of the severity of the effect and an estimate of the expected probability of its occurance for a predetermined time period assumed for analysis
      2. RPN=SxP | where : S= severity, an estimate oh how strongly the effects of the failure will affect the system or user
        |p = probability of occurence, lkelihood that the failure effect will occur
      3. RPN= SxOxD | where: S= severity as described above | O: probability of occurence |D: detectability, identify and eliminate the failure before the system or customer is affected
      4. RPN used to prioritise in addressing the mitigation of failure modes
      5. if similar or identical rpn, high severity numbers are addressed first
      6. high priority is assigned to high rpn
      7. rpn exceeding a defined threshold are not acceptable
      8. high importance is given to the high severity numbers

      5.3.3 Relationship between FMECA and risk analysis

      • FMECA usually results in a relative ranking of the contribution to the overall risk
      • risk analysis for high risk systems generally aims at risk acceptability
      • high risk effects is recognised it is adviced that a probabilistic risk analysis(PRA) should be used in preference to FMECA
      • probabilistic risk analysis, where also more influential parameters (and their interactions) can be taken into account , eg:exposure time,probability of avoidancej,latency failures , fault detecttion mechanisms

      other considerations

      common-cause failures

      in a reliability analysis,it is not sufficient to consider only random and independaent failure. some common cause failures(CCF)can occur, that causes system performance degradation or failure through simultaneous deficiency in several system components.

      the ccf will cause more than one item to fail simultaneously.

      • design: software,rating
      • manufacturing: batch related component flaws
      • environmental: electrical interferencing, temperature cycling,vibrations;
      • human factors: incorrect operating or maintenance actions;

      a ccf is that result of an event that, because of logical dependancies, causes a coincidence of failure states in two or more components(excluding secondary failures caused by the effects of a primary failues).

      a combination of several methods:
      • system modeling
      • physical analysis of components
      • functional diversity:redundant branches or parts of the system performing the same function are not identical and have different failure modes
      • physical seperation to eliminate influence of environmental or EMI stress causing CCF

      7.2 benefits of FMEA

      1. avoid costly modifications by the early identification of design deficiencies
      2. identify failures which when they occur alone or in combination
      3. to determine the need for the design methods for reliability improvement( redundancy,operational stresses,fail safe,component selection adn de-rating,etc.);
      4. logic model required to evaluate the probability or rate of occurence of anamolous operating conditions of the system in preperation for the criticality analysis
      5. disclose safety and product liability problem areas, or non compliance with regulatory requirements
      6. ensure that the development test programme can detect potential failure modes
      7. focus upon key areas in which to concentrate quality control,inspection and manufacturing proceess controls
      8. assisr in defin;ing various aspectsof the feneral preventive maintenance strategyand schedule
      9. facilitate or support the determination of test criteria, test planning and diagnostic procedures for example:performance testing,reliability testing;
      10. support the design fault isolationsequences and to support the planning for alternative modes of operation and reconfiguration
      11. designers with an understanding of the factors which indluence the reliability of the system;
      12. final document that is proof of the fact that(and od the extent to which) care has been taken to ensure that the design will meet its specification in service

      7.3 limitations and deficiencies of FMEA

      1. efficient when applied with analysis of elements that cause failure of the entire system or of a major function of the system.
      2. FMEA may be difficult and tedious for the complex systems that have multiple functions
      3. difficulties may be increased by the eistence of a number of possible operating modes
      4. relationshi[ps between individual or groups of failure modes or causes of failure modes cannot be effectively presented in FMEA
      5. deficiency becomes even more pronounced in view of software/hardware interactions, where independency assumption does not apply
      6. when adding the human interactions with hardware and the modeling their interdependancy
      7. interrelationship scenarios are far better modelled using the approach of failure mode analysis with the FTA tool (IEC 60300-3-1, edition 2).
      8. restricted to relating teo levels only in the hierarchical structure.for example,it is relatively straightforward task to identify failre modes of items and to determine their effects on the assembly.
      9. however succesful multilevel FMEA are often carried out

        methods: deductive FTA

        • it is often difficult to identify whether the inductice or deductive aproach
        • where levels of risk are identified in industrial facilities and systems, the deductive approach is preffered
        • dulring the early design stages ,where only functions, general system structure and subsystems have been defined ,succesfull performance of the system can be depicted bya reliability block diagram or by a faukyre path by a fault tree
        • fta traces the low level causes of a postulated high level failure
        • deficiencies of an FMEA is its inability to view interaction and dynamics of failure mode occurences in a system
        • logic of coincidence and alternative events causing undesirable consequences
        • if the system is based mainly on series logic, with new redundancies and few functions, then FTA is an unnecessary complicated way
        1. FMEA is appropriate when comprehensive knowledge of the failure characteristics of an item is required.
        2. FMEA is more appropriate for smaller systems, modules or assemblies.
        3. FMEA is an essential tool at the research and development or design stage when unacceptable effects of failures need to be identified and solutions found.
        4. FMEA can be necessary for items that are of innovatory design and their failure characteristics cannot be known from previous operational experience.
        5. FMEA is usually more applicable to systems having large numbers of components to be considered that are related by predominantly series failure logic.
        6. FTA is generally more suitable for the analysis of multiple failure modes and dependency involving complex failure logic and redundancy.
